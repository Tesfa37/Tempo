# Tempo — Judge Q&A Curriculum
## CICDC 2026 Digital Marketing Strategies

**Preparation standard:** Both partners can answer any question in this document cold, with specific data, in 60-90 seconds.

**Structure:**
- 6 question categories
- 30 total scenarios
- Each with: likely question wording, ideal partner to lead, model answer, fallback data, known traps

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

1. Read through once end-to-end
2. Partner up: Bityana reads the question, Tesfa answers. Then swap.
3. No notes during practice. Force cold recall.
4. Time each answer with a phone timer.
5. Record at least one full Q&A session on video and watch it back.
6. The ordering below is roughly most-likely to least-likely, based on research into past CICDC DMS rounds.

## CATEGORY 1 — COMPETITIVE DIFFERENTIATION

### Q1. "How are you different from Tommy Hilfiger Adaptive?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Three differentiators. One, we're adaptive-first by construction, not a line extension of a mainstream brand. Tommy started with Tommy and added Adaptive; we started with disabled customers and built out. Two, we're sustainable with verified certifications — GOTS organic cotton, Fair Trade labor — which Tommy has no equivalent for. Three, and most importantly, we don't hide our adaptive features. Disability design critic Liz Jackson has written directly about Tommy's 'sartorial assimilation' problem — the magnetic closures are hidden so the garment looks 'normal,' which quietly tells disabled customers their bodies should look normal too. Tempo rejects that. Our features are visible and celebrated."
**Known trap:** Do not sound anti-Tommy. Frame it as "we learned from where Tommy succeeded and where disabled community critics pointed out gaps."

---

### Q2. "What stops Tommy Hilfiger from adding DPP and sustainable materials and just crushing you in 18 months?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "Three things. First, Tommy Adaptive is a line extension inside PVH Corp's nine-billion-dollar portfolio. The incentive to invest in a 0.1% subsegment isn't there. Second, their brand positioning makes the authenticity pivot almost impossible — they've already been publicly critiqued for ablesaviorism, and overcorrecting would admit they had a problem. Third, even if they did all of this, our moat isn't DPP or materials. Our moat is community trust. We start with disabled advisors as co-designers, credited and compensated. Tommy can copy features. They can't retroactively earn the trust."
**Known trap:** Do not claim Tommy *can't* compete. Claim Tommy *won't*, and back it with specific business logic.

---

### Q3. "What's your answer to Nike FlyEase?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Nike FlyEase is a footwear product, not a brand. We're not competing on shoes. More broadly, Nike got criticized for not using the word 'disabled' in their own launch marketing — Liz Jackson and Jaipreet Virdi wrote in Slate that Nike 'erased' the community the shoe was designed for. We're building the opposite brand. Disabled is not a dirty word for us; it's our customer."
**Known trap:** Don't spend more than 45 seconds here. Nike isn't a real competitive threat for Tempo, and dwelling implies otherwise.

---

### Q4. "Why hasn't someone already done this?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "Actually, three partial versions exist. IZ Adaptive has disabled-led authenticity but no sustainability and no tech. June Adaptive has caregiver focus but no sustainability and basic e-commerce. Joe & Bella serves seniors beautifully but isn't fashion-forward. Each proves a single pillar. Nobody has combined them because doing so requires simultaneous expertise in adaptive design, sustainable sourcing, emerging regulatory tech, and caregiver UX — four domains that historically don't overlap. The EU's 2027 DPP mandate is the forcing function that makes the fourth pillar newly credible. We're building at the exact moment these four converge."
**Known trap:** "Nobody has done this" is wrong and a judge will catch it. "Partial versions exist" is correct and signals thorough research.

---

### Q5. "What about IZ Adaptive or June Adaptive — aren't those more authentic than you?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Izzy Camilleri and June Adaptive are both authentic brands with real pedigree — I have deep respect for both. We're not positioned against them. We're positioned against the gap all three of us share: none of us until now has combined authenticity with the four-pillar stack. Our relationship with IZ and June is closer to collaborator than competitor. We'd actively partner with Open Style Lab, which IZ's founder has worked with. The adaptive fashion market is not zero-sum. Every disabled person served by IZ or June is a customer we don't need to acquire from scratch."
**Known trap:** Don't position Tempo as superior to authentic disabled-led brands. That's a fast authenticity loss.

## CATEGORY 2 — AUTHENTICITY AND THE ABLESAVIORISM TRAP

### Q6. "Neither of you has a disability. How do you justify building this brand?"
**Lead:** Bityana (the personal origin partner)
**Model answer:** "Fair question, and one we expected. Tesfa and I both worked in home care before consulting. Not as executives visiting a facility — as paid workers doing the daily dressing, bathing, and transport. We know what's broken from the floor up, not the boardroom. That's not the same as lived experience as a disabled person, and we're clear about that. Which is why our brand is structured with at least 30 percent disabled representation on our advisory board, all compensated, all credited on our About page. We don't speak for the community. We build with them, and we hand the mic over when the question is about lived experience."
**Known trap:** Do NOT claim home care is equivalent to disability. Acknowledge the distinction and pivot to structural safeguards.

---

### Q7. "What's ablesaviorism and why should we care?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "It's a critique named by disability writers like Liz Jackson and Alice Wong for the pattern of non-disabled founders treating disability as inspiration rather than expertise. Classic example: a brand founder has a family member with a disability, designs a product, launches it with a narrative centered on the founder's heartwarming journey, and disabled people end up absent from their own product story. Why you should care: it's the fastest way to lose the community this brand needs. Disability is a political and cultural identity, not a tragic plot device. We're building Tempo under the principle 'designed with, not for.'"
**Known trap:** Know the term. If you can't define ablesaviorism cold, the rest of your authenticity argument collapses.

---

### Q8. "How do you know you're not accidentally doing ablesaviorism?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "We can't know in the abstract. We build structural accountability. Our advisory board of disabled consultants reviews our marketing copy before it ships, our product designs before they're manufactured, and our press narrative before any interview. We publish their names and their roles. We pay them. We cite them. And we commit to being publicly correctable — if a disabled writer or advocate calls out a misstep, we don't defend; we adjust. The only way to not be an ablesavior is to not treat the critique as a threat to your ego."
**Known trap:** Do not get defensive. This question is a test of humility.

---

### Q9. "What happens if a disabled advocate publicly criticizes Tempo?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "We treat it as data, not attack. Our crisis playbook has three steps. One, within 24 hours we acknowledge the critique in plain language — no 'we hear you' corporate-speak. Two, we publicly commit to a specific response, whether that's a product change, a hiring change, or a policy change, with a timeline. Three, we follow up 30 and 90 days later to show the work. This is how trust gets built in this community — not by avoiding mistakes, but by handling them with visible integrity."
**Known trap:** Avoid saying "we'd never." Claim process, not perfection.

---

### Q10. "The name 'Tempo' — where does it come from and does the community approve?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Tempo is a direct reference to 'crip time,' a concept from disability scholars like Alison Kafer and Ellen Samuels. Crip time says the clock should bend to the disabled body, not the other way around. When we workshopped brand names with our disabled advisors, the ones referencing euphemism — 'differently-abled,' 'abled,' 'capable' — all tested badly. The ones referencing the community's own intellectual traditions tested best. Tempo is an in-community reference. A disability studies-literate judge will recognize it. A non-specialist judge will simply hear 'clothes that move at your pace,' which is on-message either way."
**Known trap:** If the judge has NOT heard of crip time, do NOT make them feel uninformed. Frame the explanation as "it's a concept from disability scholarship" without lecturing.

## CATEGORY 3 — MARKETING STRATEGY AND CHANNELS

### Q11. "Why these specific digital channels?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "Channel choice is demographic, not trendy. Our primary customer is 22-to-45, digitally native, and community-connected. That's Instagram and TikTok for discovery, specifically disabled creator content not influencer affiliate. Our secondary customer is the caregiver, 30-to-65, who trusts peer communities more than ads — that's Facebook groups, Reddit r/CaregiverSupport, AARP forums, and our own email newsletter. Our tertiary customer is the conscious consumer, Gen Z, who validates on TikTok and Instagram but converts through email. LinkedIn matters only for our B2B care-facility track. Every channel is tied to a specific customer's actual media behavior, backed by Pew and eMarketer data."
**Known trap:** "Because everyone uses Instagram" is a rubric-killer answer. Always tie channel to specific customer behavior.

---

### Q12. "What's your customer acquisition cost and how did you calculate it?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Blended CAC of $42, built from channel-specific benchmarks. Meta paid social CAC in sustainable fashion runs $38-55 per Upcounting's 2025 data. Google Shopping runs $30-45 for niche categories. Email and organic run under $10 effectively. Our expected mix is 40% paid social, 25% paid search, 15% influencer and creator partnerships, 20% organic and email. That blend produces $42. We've modeled scenarios at $35, $42, and $55 to stress-test viability. Even at $55, our LTV-to-CAC ratio is 5.2x, well above the DTC-healthy threshold of 3x."
**Known trap:** Don't just give a number. Show the calculation, show the sensitivity.

---

### Q13. "How do you address greenwashing skepticism?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "Three mechanisms. First, certification-backed claims only — GOTS for organic cotton, Fair Trade for labor, Bluesign for dyes. No self-declared 'eco-friendly' language. Second, the Digital Product Passport itself IS our greenwashing defense. Every customer can scan and see the factory, the materials, the certifications, the carbon footprint. Third, we publish our supply chain map publicly. Greenwashing exists because transparency is opt-in. DPP makes it default."
**Known trap:** Don't dismiss the concern. Greenwashing is a real problem in sustainable fashion and the judge knows it.

---

### Q14. "Why should we trust your 3.4% conversion rate projection?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Three layers of evidence. Shopify's 2025 DTC fashion benchmark report puts sustainable fashion at 2.3-3.1%. Adaptive fashion categories index 0.5-0.7 percentage points higher, per Grips Intelligence data on MagnaReady and Silverts traffic, because adaptive shoppers are higher-intent — they're not browsing, they're problem-solving. So the adaptive benchmark range is 2.8-3.8%. We're modeling 3.4%, at the upper range but not the ceiling. If we miss and hit 2.8%, our financial model still clears positive LTV-to-CAC at 5.1x."
**Known trap:** Don't promise the top of the range without explaining what happens if you miss.

---

### Q15. "What's your year-one revenue projection based on?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "$480,000, built bottoms-up. Four thousand customers at $120 AOV equals $480K. Four thousand customers, at 3.4% conversion, requires roughly 118,000 site visitors. At $42 CAC, that's $168,000 in marketing spend, which is our budget. Every input is benchmarked. If we miss on traffic, we miss on revenue proportionally. We've modeled a downside case at $320K and an upside at $620K. The middle is defensible."
**Known trap:** "Why $480K specifically" needs to reveal the calculation chain. A round number without math is a red flag.

## CATEGORY 4 — TECHNOLOGY AND DPP

### Q16. "What exactly is a Digital Product Passport and why does it matter?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "A DPP is a machine-readable digital record attached to a physical product via QR code or NFC. It contains product identity, materials, supply chain data, sustainability metrics, and care instructions. The EU's Ecodesign Regulation — ESPR 2024/1781 — makes DPPs mandatory for textiles starting with a delegated act expected in late 2026 and enforcement in 2028. For Tempo specifically, DPP isn't just compliance — it's a product feature. Our customers need to know fiber composition for allergy safety, material traceability for sensory sensitivities, and sterilization compatibility for care facility use. For mainstream fashion, DPP is overhead. For adaptive, it's core."
**Known trap:** Know the acronyms. ESPR. GS1. CIRPASS. If you can't name them, the technical credibility argument collapses.

---

### Q17. "How much does implementing DPP cost for a brand your size?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Fifteen thousand dollars in year one, based on current market pricing from DPP service providers like Avelero and Kezzler. That's $1-10 per passport depending on volume, plus setup and integration. For context, that's less than a quarter-page ad in a mid-tier lifestyle magazine. The bigger cost is data — gathering supply chain information from tier-one suppliers — but as a direct-to-consumer brand with a single manufacturing partner, we have that data already. For a brand that scaled from zero, we're advantaged. Brands with complex legacy supply chains will spend millions. We'll spend fifteen thousand."
**Known trap:** Don't treat DPP as free. Be honest about the cost and explain why it's worth it.

---

### Q18. "What happens if the EU DPP deadline slips?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "It won't change our strategy. The DPP serves our customers regardless of EU regulation — allergen data and sterilization info are operational necessities for caregivers, not compliance checkboxes. If the EU deadline slips from 2028 to 2030, we still ship the same feature. If it accelerates, we still ship the same feature. Our architecture doesn't depend on regulatory timing; it depends on customer need. Regulatory tailwind is a gift, not a dependency."
**Known trap:** Don't bet the brand on government timing. This question tests whether you did.

---

### Q19. "How does your AI Fit Concierge actually work? Isn't it just a chatbot?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "It's a Claude Sonnet API integration, not a canned chatbot. We trained it on our catalog metadata, our fit guidance, and on the real vocabulary caregivers and disabled customers use — including condition-specific terminology like 'seated cut' and 'post-stroke.' When a customer describes their situation, the Concierge returns specific product recommendations with specific reasoning — not 'have you considered our products.' Stated differently: it works the way a knowledgeable sales associate with deep disability-community fluency would work. That kind of associate doesn't exist at scale in fashion retail. The AI fills the gap."
**Known trap:** "It's just GPT" is a loss. Demonstrate that it's trained, contextualized, and specific.

---

### Q20. "What if the AI gives a bad recommendation?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "Three safeguards. One, the Concierge always offers a 'talk to a human' handoff — we staff a small care team trained on adaptive fit. Two, we monitor response quality via user feedback thumbs and log analysis, retraining the system prompts weekly. Three, every recommendation is advisory, not transactional — it suggests, the customer clicks through to the real product page where they make the purchase decision. AI failure mode is 'unhelpful suggestion,' not 'wrong product shipped.'"
**Known trap:** AI hype is a Q&A trap. Show that you thought about failure modes.

## CATEGORY 5 — FINANCIAL DEFENSIBILITY

### Q21. "What's your gross margin and how do you defend it?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "55% gross margin target. DTC sustainable fashion benchmarks from Shopify and Triple Whale cluster at 50-60% for brands using certified materials. We're mid-range because our materials — GOTS organic cotton, Fair Trade labor — add 12-18% to COGS vs. conventional fashion, but our average order value of $120 supports the premium. Our cost structure: roughly $54 COGS per unit, $6 fulfillment, $60 margin. Scaling to 8,000 units in year two drops COGS to $48 through volume breaks. Margin rises naturally without price changes."
**Known trap:** A margin without a COGS structure is suspicious. Show the components.

---

### Q22. "Why this marketing budget specifically? It seems high."
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "35% of revenue in year one, which aligns with DTC launch benchmarks. Industry convention is to spend aggressively in years one and two to build a customer base, then drop marketing-to-revenue ratio to 15-20% by year three as retention and organic discovery scale. Our customer lifetime value is $285. At $42 CAC, every dollar acquiring a customer returns $6.80 over that customer's lifespan. Spending less in year one would mean fewer customers to earn LTV from. The math rewards front-loading."
**Known trap:** High marketing spend is a red flag if you don't defend it with retention economics.

---

### Q23. "What happens if Meta ad costs spike 30%?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Our paid-social CAC rises from $42 to roughly $54 blended, which drops our LTV-to-CAC ratio to 5.3x — still well above healthy. We've modeled that scenario. Our defensive move is to shift budget from paid social to partnership and community. Creator partnerships and condition-specific SEO content both have higher activation energy but much lower marginal CAC. At a $55 CAC equilibrium, we'd cut paid social share from 40% to 25% and reinvest in content and community programs. The brand survives."
**Known trap:** Don't be rigid. Digital marketing is an optimization problem, not a fixed plan.

---

### Q24. "When do you break even?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "Month 14 on a cumulative basis. Year one burns about $32,000 after $480K revenue minus $168K marketing, $264K COGS, and $80K operations. Year two at projected $1.1M revenue — roughly 130% growth based on DTC sustainable fashion category norms — produces $150K cumulative positive after month 14. By month 24 we're at $180K in retained earnings, which funds the EU expansion. This is a capital-efficient model; we don't need a second funding round to reach profitability."
**Known trap:** "When do you break even" is often a lazy question but the judge wants proof of real modeling. Give the month, not the year.

---

### Q25. "What's your exit strategy?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "We're building Tempo for operational profitability, not a strategic sale. But if the question is about long-term optionality: in five years, the adaptive fashion market will be $24B globally and DPP-native brands will be scarce. Strategic acquirers would include PVH Corp (Tommy Hilfiger parent) or Kering's sustainability arm. More interesting to us is continued independence — the adaptive market is still early enough that a well-run independent brand can capture 2-4% market share sustainably. We're not planning to sell. We're planning to last."
**Known trap:** Don't pitch an acquisition. Judges score that as lack of conviction.

## CATEGORY 6 — SCENARIO AND CHALLENGE QUESTIONS

### Q26. "What's the single biggest risk to this brand?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "Authenticity erosion. If we grow faster than our disabled advisory board and community relationships can scale with us, we become the thing we're critiquing. This is more dangerous than CAC inflation or regulatory delay. Our mitigation: we've committed to a 1-to-500 ratio of compensated disabled advisors to active customers, and we cap our own marketing growth if the advisory structure can't keep pace. Authenticity is a constraint on our growth curve, and we're explicit about that."
**Known trap:** A risk-free answer is a red flag. Show that you know where the landmines are.

---

### Q27. "What if you fail?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Two scenarios. If we fail on the business, we've still produced real product for real disabled customers and open-sourced our DPP implementation, which the community benefits from. If we fail on the authenticity dimension, that's catastrophic and we'd shut down rather than become the brand we set out to replace. We'd rather not exist than exist poorly in this space. That's not a marketing line — it's a design principle. The exit door is built in."
**Known trap:** Judges want to see humility and stakes. Not "we won't fail."

---

### Q28. "Pitch us on why we, as this hypothetical sustainable fashion client, should hire you over another consulting team?"
**Lead:** Bityana (sell), Tesfa (close)
**Model answer (B):** "Three reasons. One, our home care experience gives us primary-source understanding of the adaptive customer that consulting teams typically don't have. Two, our strategy integrates across four pillars that no competitor combines, creating a defensible moat rather than feature parity. Three, our plan is operational today — not strategic theater. You could execute our first 90 days without additional research."
**Model answer (T):** "But the real reason is simpler. You're not hiring a digital marketing strategy. You're hiring a team who understands that the people you're designing clothes for have been consulted, co-designed with, and credited. That's a different operating system. And in this category, it's the only operating system that works."
**Known trap:** This is the selling-the-sale question. Both partners speak. Close decisively.

---

### Q29. "If you had to cut one pillar of the four, which one goes?"
**Lead:** Tesfa
**Model answer:** "Honestly? None of them can go, which is why they're called pillars and not features. But if you're testing whether I understand dependencies: the Caregiver-First UX is the most recent addition to the adaptive fashion category — you could ship year one without it and add it in year two. Sustainable materials and adaptive design are bedrock. DPP is the regulatory moat. Caregiver UX is the market-expanding pillar. If I were forced to sequence rather than cut, caregiver UX would arrive in month six rather than month one. But I'd argue against cutting. The question is whether you build a complete thing that takes longer, or an incomplete thing faster. We chose complete."
**Known trap:** Don't refuse to engage. Show you've thought about the tradeoff.

---

### Q30. "What have I not asked you that I should have?"
**Lead:** Bityana
**Model answer:** "Two things. First, you haven't asked about our international strategy — Year 2 is a direct EU launch enabled by our DPP infrastructure, which puts us in a €600 billion market with a meaningful head start on competitors scrambling for 2028 compliance. Second, you haven't asked about the disability data moat — as our AI Fit Concierge handles thousands of conversations about body types, conditions, and fit preferences, we accumulate a dataset that no mainstream brand can replicate, because their customers don't share that information at that depth. Those are the two questions we'd be asked by a sharp board member, and the answers are material to long-term valuation."
**Known trap:** This is the "show me you think like an operator" question. The answer should reveal strategic depth, not just information.

## REHEARSAL PROTOCOL

**Round 1 (minimum):** Both partners answer all 30 questions. One partner asks, one answers. Swap. 2 hours.

**Round 2:** Random sampling. One partner picks 10 questions by number, the other must answer cold. Reverse. 90 minutes.

**Round 3:** External reviewer. Your professor, a CodePath mentor, or a trusted colleague plays judge. They pick questions, you both respond. 60 minutes.

**Round 4 (night before competition):** Lightning round. One partner asks, other answers in 45 seconds or less. All 30 questions. 45 minutes.

**Recording requirement:** At least one full Q&A round recorded on video. Watch it back. Note: pace, eye contact, verbal tics ("um," "like," "you know"), and handoffs between partners.

---

## RED-FLAG BEHAVIORS TO ELIMINATE

- Answering every question yourself when your partner should lead
- Saying "that's a great question" before any answer (it's not; it's a test)
- Starting with "so" or "basically" (signals weakness)
- Saying "honestly" twice in the same answer (signals dishonesty)
- Breaking eye contact to look at your slides or laptop
- Defending a weak answer when the judge has clearly moved on
- Letting the other partner fumble without stepping in
