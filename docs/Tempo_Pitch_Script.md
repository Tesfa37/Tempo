**T:** "And I'm Tesfa Desta. We're co-founders of Desta and Yishak Consulting."

## ORIGIN + PROBLEM (1:00-2:30)

*Slide 2: "The 15-Minute Morning" — large centered text, no graphics yet*

**T:** "A seated client. Sixty-three years old. Post-stroke. Right-side paralysis. The pants cost four ninety-nine at Walmart. They had a button, a zipper, and a loop that was designed for someone with two functioning hands."

**T:** "It took fifteen minutes to put them on. Fifteen minutes, twice a day, every day. That's seven and a half hours a month. Ninety hours a year. Just to put on pants."

*Slide 3: The number "90" fills the screen, then transitions to "90 hours per year per person"*

**B:** "And while Tesfa was in one home doing that math, I was in a different home watching a caregiver throw out her back lifting a client whose shirt didn't have the right opening. She was making fourteen dollars an hour. She couldn't take a week off."

**B:** "Fashion wasn't the problem. Fashion had just never been the solution."

*Slide 4: "1.3 billion people worldwide live with disability." Transitions to include "53 million US family caregivers." Transitions to include "3.5 million paid home health aides." Final state shows all three numbers stacked.*

**T:** "One point three billion disabled people globally. Sixty-one million in the US. Behind each one, a caregiver. Fifty-three million family caregivers. Three and a half million paid aides. Together, the adaptive-fashion customer base is larger than the population of Germany and France combined."

**B:** "And the fashion industry has been designing for everyone else."

## MARKET + WHITESPACE (2:30-4:30)

*Slide 5: Market sizing chart — $17.7B global adaptive market 2024 growing to $26-28B by 2030 at 6-7% CAGR*

**B:** "The global adaptive clothing market is seventeen point seven billion dollars today, projected to reach twenty-eight billion by 2030. The US share is about five point seven billion. And here's something most teams will miss: adaptive-fashion consumers have better unit economics than mainstream fashion. Lower return rates, higher conversion, higher email engagement. When someone needs clothes that actually fit their body, they don't casually shop. They commit."

*Slide 6: Competitive matrix — 4-pillar grid showing Tommy Adaptive, Nike FlyEase, IZ Adaptive, June Adaptive, Joe & Bella, Silverts, and Tempo across the four pillars: Adaptive-First, Sustainable Materials, DPP Technology, Caregiver Experience. Only Tempo has all four filled.*

**T:** "Here's every major competitor in the space against four pillars. Tommy Hilfiger Adaptive has scale, but zero sustainability story and has been publicly critiqued by disabled writer Liz Jackson for what she calls 'ablesaviorism.' Nike FlyEase refused to use the word 'disabled' in its own launch marketing. IZ Adaptive has authenticity but no sustainability and no tech. Joe and Bella has caregivers but no sustainability. June Adaptive comes closest, but no DPP, no tech layer."

**B:** "Every brand in this space has picked two pillars. We're building on four."

**T:** "And we're doing it with specific technical credibility. The EU Ecodesign Regulation passed in July 2024 makes Digital Product Passports mandatory for textiles by 2027, with full enforcement in 2028. Most brands are going to scramble. We're starting DPP-native. On day one."

## BRAND + POSITIONING (4:30-5:30)

*Slide 7: Brand reveal. Tempo wordmark appears, then tagline: "Clothes that move at your pace." Clean, high-contrast, accessible typography.*

**T:** "The brand is Tempo. The name is deliberate."

*(pause — this is the moment)*

**T:** "In the disability community, there's a concept called 'crip time.' It says the clock should bend to the body, not the body to the clock. The fashion industry has spent a hundred and fifty years making bodies bend to clothes. Tempo does the opposite."

*Slide 8: Four-pillar positioning visual — Adaptive Design / Sustainable Materials / Digital Product Passport / Caregiver-First. Each pillar gets 2-3 supporting words.*

**B:** "Four pillars, all non-negotiable. Adaptive design that's visible, not hidden — no pretending our customers don't exist. Sustainable materials with GOTS and Fair Trade certification because the people who buy adaptive clothes are the same people who taught the world to care about allergens. Digital Product Passports because our customers need provenance more than anyone, not less. And caregivers as a first-class design target, not a footnote."

## DIGITAL ECOSYSTEM (5:30-9:30)

*Slide 9: Website homepage screenshot*

**B:** "Let's walk through the digital ecosystem. Starting with the website."

*Stage direction: B opens the actual site on the laptop, turns screen toward judge. Even if only the hero page loads, the motion of opening a live URL matters.*

**B:** "WCAG 2.1 AA compliant from the ground up. Not added later. Screen-reader navigation, keyboard-only flows, adjustable text, full captioning on every product video. Our accessibility statement is a first-class page, not a footer link."

*Slide 10: Product detail page with Fit Concierge chat*

**T:** "The AI Fit Concierge is a live Claude-powered assistant. A customer describes their body, their condition, their morning routine, and the Concierge recommends from our catalog with specific reasoning. It's trained on our product knowledge and on the vocabulary caregivers actually use."

*(If demo is live: T types a quick prompt into the Concierge and shows the response. If not, static screenshot with representative dialogue.)*

*Slide 11: DPP passport page — a real product's QR code visible, linking to the passport URL*

**T:** "Every product has a Digital Product Passport. Not as a compliance checkbox for 2027 but as a product feature. Our customers need to know fiber composition for allergies. Caregivers need to know sterilization compatibility for care facilities. Material traceability for sensory sensitivities. A standard fashion DPP is compliance. For Tempo, it's mission-critical product data."

*Optional: B hands the judge a phone, T pulls out a physical business card with a QR code that opens the live DPP passport page. Judge scans, sees live page. This is the money moment.*

*Slide 12: Caregiver Mode toggle — product listing page transforms when toggle is flipped*

**B:** "Caregiver Mode. One toggle, and the entire site changes. Products filter by condition — dementia-friendly, post-stroke, wheelchair-seated, sensory-sensitive. Time-to-dress estimates appear on each garment. Peer reviews from caregivers show separately from reviews by the person wearing the clothes. Bulk purchase workflow for care facilities. No competitor has this."

*Slide 13: Social + content strategy dashboard*

**T:** "Content and social. Our primary channel is Instagram and TikTok, but not with influencer affiliate contracts. We partner with disabled creators as co-designers, paid as advisors, credited as collaborators. User-generated content under hashtag 'TempoStyle' with a creator fund specifically for disabled creators. Our blog is condition-specific buying guides — 'Adaptive Clothing for Multiple Sclerosis,' 'Dementia-Friendly Dressing Guide' — each one is SEO bait for high-intent search and each one gets reviewed by a disabled advisor before it ships."

*Slide 14: Email lifecycle + partnership map*

**B:** "Email marketing runs three distinct lifecycles. Adaptive consumer, caregiver, and B2B for care facilities. Each has its own tone, cadence, and content. Open rates in adaptive categories are twenty-eight to thirty-five percent, nearly double mainstream fashion. Partnerships with AARP, the Christopher Reeve Foundation, and Open Style Lab are structured as content co-creation, not sponsorship. Credibility cannot be bought. It has to be earned."

*Slide 16: Final slide — Tempo wordmark, one line of text*

**B:** "Our ask is simple. Hire Desta and Yishak Consulting. Tempo launches in EU markets directly in year two. By the time every fashion brand scrambles to comply with the 2027 DPP mandate, we'll have eighteen months of operational data."

Today we're asking: what if those fifteen minutes could be five? What if the clothes also told the story of where they came from? And what if the brand that built this was actually designed with disabled people, not for them?"

**B:** "That's Tempo. Clothes that move at your pace."

*(Both partners step back slightly, signaling end of prepared presentation)*

## Transition phrases (memorize these)

These are the verbal bridges between sections. Practice until seamless.

- End of Hook → Origin: "Let us tell you about one morning."
- End of Origin → Market: "That morning scales. Here's the math."
- End of Market → Whitespace: "Every brand in this space has picked two pillars."
- End of Whitespace → Brand: "So we built on four. The brand is Tempo."
- End of Brand → Digital: "Let's walk through how this shows up digitally."
- End of each Digital section → next: "That's [section]. Next, [section]." (keep it tight)
- End of Digital → ROI: "All of this adds up to a business."
- End of ROI → Close: "Our ask is simple."
- End of Close → Q&A: "We'd love to take your questions."

- Lead with the home care story. You own the emotional opening.

- Return to lead for the brand reveal (slide 7). This is the rhythm peak.
- Close strong. The final callback to the 15-minute morning is yours.

**Bityana:**
- You're the business voice. Market, ecosystem, and the structural thinking.
- Use hand gestures for the numbers. 1.3 billion is a physical gesture. 53 million is a physical gesture.
- The live demo is yours. Drive the laptop confidently. If it lags, smile and say "this is why we deploy to the edge."
- The final tagline ("clothes that move at your pace") is yours. Land it.

**Transitions between you two:**
- Always touch the other partner's name in your last sentence of your section. "...and Tesfa will walk you through the brand." This is professional and shows collaboration.
- Never turn your back to the judge. When switching who's leading, the seated partner stands slightly, takes the pointer or laptop, and the previous speaker sits down or steps back.

## If something goes wrong

**Demo fails:** "Rather than troubleshoot, let me show you screenshots of this working, and then I'll send you a live link." Move on. Do not spend more than 20 seconds on technical failures.

**You forget a number:** "The exact figure is in our leave-behind, but the order of magnitude is..." then give the approximate. Never lie. Never stall.

**Judge interrupts mid-presentation with a question:** Answer it briefly (30 seconds max), then "I'm going to hold the rest of that for Q&A so we can cover the full strategy. Where we were..." — resume exactly where you left off.

**Partner freezes:** The other partner picks up smoothly. "What [name] is about to show you is..." then you present that section. Practice this scenario specifically.

**You go over time:** Cut the Digital Ecosystem subsections ruthlessly. Skip slide 13 or 14 if needed. Never cut the close. The callback is scored.

---

## What NOT to say

Never in this pitch:
- "Differently-abled," "special needs," "handicapable," "people suffering from"
- "We were inspired by" (triggers ablesaviorism critique)
- "This makes things easier for disabled people" (use: "This respects how disabled people already move")
- "We created this FOR the disability community" (use: "We built this WITH disabled advisors")
- "Everyone deserves" (charity framing; use business framing)
- "It's the right thing to do" (reduces brand to morality; use: "It's the right business opportunity")
